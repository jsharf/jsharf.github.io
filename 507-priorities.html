11 U.S.C. § 507 — Priority Checker

Determine whether a claim or expense has priority status under the Bankruptcy Code.
Not legal advice. We are not lawyers. This is a good-faith attempt to implement the text of 11 U.S.C. § 507 as a rules engine, with disambiguating questions and case-law caveats surfaced where leading decisions control the outcome. It does not exhaustively model every circuit split, it doesn't apply equitable doctrines beyond the explicit § 507(a)(8) tolling, and it treats user check-boxes as ground truth rather than evaluating the underlying facts. Dollar caps reflect the April 1, 2025 adjustments. Verify everything against the current statute, current case law in your jurisdiction, and any subsequent triennial adjustments before relying on any output.

Claim Type
Domestic Support Details

How courts read this

Courts apply a function-not-form test: a debt's label ("alimony," "support," "property settlement") is not controlling. They look at the parties' intent, the structure of the obligation, and whether it actually serves a support function. Periodic payments that terminate on death/remarriage and address need usually qualify; lump-sum equalization payments tied to property typically do not. Property settlements get no priority and are nondischargeable in chapter 7 only via § 523(a)(15).
  • In re Sampson, 997 F.2d 717 (10th Cir. 1993) — three-factor test (intent, function, surrounding circumstances).
  • In re Brody, 3 F.3d 35 (2d Cir. 1993) — periodic payments terminating on death = support.
  • In re Werthen, 329 F.3d 269 (1st Cir. 2003) — even a lump sum can be support if structured to address need.
Administrative Expense Details

How courts read this

"Actual and necessary" under § 503(b)(1)(A) is a heavily litigated standard. The leading rule (Jartran) requires that (i) the consideration arose from a transaction with the debtor-in-possession (i.e., post-petition), and (ii) it directly benefited the estate. Pre-petition obligations don't qualify even if they're paid post-petition. The Reading exception covers post-petition torts.
  • In re Jartran, Inc., 732 F.2d 584 (7th Cir. 1984) — two-prong post-petition transaction test.
  • Reading Co. v. Brown, 391 U.S. 471 (1968) — post-petition tort claims get admin priority.
  • In re DAK Industries, 66 F.3d 1091 (9th Cir. 1995) — economic-realities test for whether contract is true-lease vs. sale.
Gap Claim Details

How courts read this

"Ordinary course" requires both dimensions: a transaction matching the debtor's historical practice (vertical) and typical of the broader industry (horizontal). Routine vendor payments usually qualify; one-off financings, asset sales, or insider transfers usually don't.
  • In re Roth American, Inc., 975 F.2d 949 (3d Cir. 1992) — articulates the horizontal/vertical framework.
  • In re Dant & Russell, Inc., 853 F.2d 700 (9th Cir. 1988) — applies the test to lease assumption.
Wage Claim Details
Cap is $17,150 per individual. Only wages earned within 180 days before the petition date OR before business operations ceased (whichever is earlier) qualify.

How courts read this

  • "Earned" ≠ paid. Wages are earned when services are rendered. The 180-day window is measured against the rendering date, not the pay date.
  • Severance: circuits split. Some allocate the entire severance amount to the date of termination (likely in-window if termination is recent); others pro-rate over the entire employment period (often pushing most of it out of the window). In re Roth American, 975 F.2d 949 (3d Cir. 1992) — pro-rata allocation. Compare In re Public Ledger, 161 F.2d 762 (3d Cir. 1947) — earned at termination.
  • Bonuses are typically pro-rated over the period earned.
  • $17,150 cap is per individual, not per claim. Aggregate the wage and benefit claims across all capacities.
  • Independent contractors are not "employees" under § 507(a)(4); their claims are general unsecured. Apply the relevant circuit's economic-reality / common-law test.
Employee Benefit Plan Details
Cap = ($17,150 × covered employees) − 4th-priority wage payments to those employees − any payments made by the estate on their behalf to other plans.

How courts read this

The Supreme Court in Howard Delivery Service v. Zurich American Ins. Co., 547 U.S. 651 (2006) read § 507(a)(5) narrowly: workers' compensation insurance premiums do not qualify as a "contribution to an employee benefit plan." The priority is meant for traditional fringe benefits (health, pension, vacation), not insurance the employer carries for its own protection. ERISA-plan claims are common; non-ERISA arrangements are scrutinized closely.
Grain / Fisherman Details
Cap is $8,450 per individual.

How courts read this

Defined terms: "grain storage facility" and "fish produce storage or processing facility" come from § 557(b). The producer/fisherman must have stored or delivered the goods to the debtor; pure cash claims for sale price typically don't qualify. This priority is rarely litigated but the statutory definitions are unforgiving.
Consumer Deposit Details
Cap is $3,800 per individual. Deposit must have been made before the petition was filed.

How courts read this

The priority is narrow — strictly construed in favor of the estate. Some courts treat gift-card balances and unfulfilled credit-card purchases as qualifying deposits; others don't. The "personal, family, or household" use test borrows from consumer-credit doctrine.
  • In re WW Warehouse, Inc., 313 B.R. 588 (Bankr. D. Del. 2004) — gift cards as § 507(a)(7) deposits.
  • Compare with cases denying priority where the buyer charged the purchase but the issuer reversed the charge — the buyer is no longer "out" the funds.
Tax Claim Details
Income tax has priority if the return was due (with extensions) within 3 years before the petition, or assessed within 240 days before the petition, or assessable post-petition. The 3-year and 240-day windows are tolled by pending OICs and prior bankruptcy stays.
Each window is extended by the duration of any OIC pending during the window plus 90 days.
Per Young v. United States, 535 U.S. 43 (2002), and the § 507(a)(8) hanging paragraph, lookback periods are tolled during prior cases plus 90 days.
Property tax has priority if incurred before the petition and last payable without penalty within 1 year before the petition.
Employer share has priority if the wages were earned within 3 years before the petition.
Excise has priority if the transaction occurred within 3 years before the petition (or the return, if required, was last due within that period).
Customs duty generally has priority if entered within 1 year before the petition (longer in certain circumstances).

How courts read this

  • Tolling matters. The hanging paragraph at the end of § 507(a)(8) extends each lookback window by the duration of any pending OIC (+90 days) and any prior bankruptcy stay (+90 days). Young v. United States, 535 U.S. 43 (2002).
  • Trust-fund taxes (withheld income tax, employee FICA share) get priority regardless of age — but the determination of who is a "responsible person" is its own body of law. Slodov v. United States, 436 U.S. 238 (1978); United States v. Energy Resources Co., 495 U.S. 545 (1990) (tax payment designation in chapter 11 plan).
  • Late or unfiled returns. A return filed more than 2 years before the petition is generally still priority, but if no return was ever filed (or only an IRS substitute), most circuits treat the tax as nondischargeable and not entitled to priority through the ordinary lookback. In re Hindenlang, 164 F.3d 1029 (6th Cir. 1999); McCoy v. Mississippi State Tax Comm'n, 666 F.3d 924 (5th Cir. 2012) (one-day-late rule).
  • Penalties. A penalty in compensation for actual pecuniary loss gets the same priority as the underlying tax; punitive penalties don't. United States v. Ron Pair Enterprises, Inc., 489 U.S. 235 (1989).
FDIC Commitment Details

How courts read this

Originally enacted post-FIRREA (1989) to give the FDIC priority for net-worth maintenance commitments made by S&L holding companies. Rarely litigated outside the bank-holding-company context.
DUI / Operation While Intoxicated Details

How courts read this

"Unlawfully intoxicated" is determined by the law of the state where the operation occurred at the time it occurred — BAC thresholds, drugged-driving rules, and watercraft definitions all vary. "Operation" is contested: most courts hold that a stationary vehicle with the engine running can qualify; some require movement. A prior conviction is strong but not strictly required — the bankruptcy court can make its own determination.
